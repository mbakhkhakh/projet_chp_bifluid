In the two-fluid model, each fluid is characterized by distinct properties, specifically density (denoted as ρ) and viscosity (denoted as μ). These fluids conform to the Navier-Stokes equations. The model is articulated through the following equations:

For the first fluid: $$ \frac{\partial u_{1}}{\partial t}+ (u_{1}.\nabla) u_{1}= -\frac{1}{\rho_{1}}\nabla p_{1}+ \frac{1}{\rho_{1}}\nabla . \mu_{1}(\nabla u_{1}+\nabla u_{1}^{T} )+ F_{s1} $$

For the second fluid: $$ \frac{\partial u_{2}}{\partial t}+ (u_{2}.\nabla) u_{2}= -\frac{1}{\rho_{2}}\nabla p_{2}+ \frac{1}{\rho_{2}}\nabla . \mu_{2}(\nabla u_{2}+\nabla u_{2}^{T} )+ F_{s2} $$

Figure 4: Bi-fluid flow problem.

To address a unified problem involving both fluids, a single momentum equation is derived utilizing new variables ρ and μ, represented as:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+(\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla) u = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \frac{1}{\rho}\nabla \cdot \mu( \nabla \mathbf{u} + \nabla \mathbf{u}^T) + F_s $$

In this unified model, (ρ, μ) are spatially varying functions dependent on the specific fluid, adopting the values (ρ₁, μ₁) in the domain of the first fluid and (ρ₂, μ₂) in the domain of the second fluid. The interface between the two fluids, denoted as Γ, is precisely delineated as the zero level-set of a scalar function ϕ(x,t), mathematically expressed as:

$$ \Gamma = \{x \,|\, \phi(x, t) = 0\} $$

The evolution of the LevelSet function ϕ is computed at each time step by resolving the associated transport equation:

$$ \dfrac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \phi = 0 $$

Initially, $ \phi(x, t = 0) = \pm d$ is designated as the signed distance function relative to the interface. Following each computational step, the values of ρ and μ are recalculated based on the updated LevelSet using the interpolation method outlined below, thereby capturing the fluid properties' spatial variations across the interface:

$$ \rho = \rho_1 + (\rho_2 - \rho_1)F(\phi) $$

$$ \mu = \mu_1 + (\mu_2 - \mu_1)F(\phi) $$
